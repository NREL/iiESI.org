Educational Opportunities

The International Institute for Energy Systems Integration strives to establish a common understanding of the very complex area of energy systems integration through internships and educational opportunities. Download a whitepaper to learn more about energy systems integration.

Courses

Past educational workshops include:

Energy Systems Integration 101 — Belgium

The Energy Systems Integration 101 workshop was held May 18–22, 2015, at KU Leuven in Belgium. This introductory course covered the concept of energy systems integration (ESI) at all scales (from residential to continental) across multiple energy (and non-energy) domains from a technical, market, and regulatory perspective. The course was designed for graduate students and practitioners working in the field to give a perspective on the state of the art in ESI.

See the workshop agenda and presentations.

Key Research Challenges of Energy Systems Integration

The Key Research Challenges of Energy Systems Integration Workshop was held March 30–31, 2015, at Imperial College London in the United Kingdom. This 1.5-day meeting discussed several benefits for linking energy domains and identified several research challenges that a more coupled energy system could address, including virtual energy storage, transmission of bulk power, and operations of electricity grids with high levels of non-synchronous generation.

Download the full workshop summary.

Energy Systems Integration 101

Energy Systems Integration 101 was held July 21–25, 2014, at the National Renewable Energy Laboratory in Golden, Colorado. The course covered the concept of energy systems integration at all scales (from residential to continental) across multiple energy (and non-energy) domains (e.g., electricity and gas nexus, electricity and water nexus, gas and transport) from technical, market, and regulatory perspectives. The course was designed to give graduate students and practitioners in the field a perspective on the state of the art in energy systems integration. Each of the first four days consisted of four hours of lectures and four hours of project work, with some hands-on numerical modeling and forecasting exercises. On the final day, each team reported on its project.

See the course agenda and presentations.
